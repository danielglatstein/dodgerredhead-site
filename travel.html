What Is Zara Aina?

Zara Aina is a Malagasy phrase that means ‘Share Life’. Broadway actors (and my professors at NYU Tisch School of the Arts) Lucas Caleb Rooney and Bryce Pinkham founded Zara Aina to help at-risk children expand their capacity for achievement through theatrical performance and storytelling. Established in 2012 with headquarters in New York, USA and Antananarivo, Madagascar, Zara Aina is a not-for-profit organization working with partners to develop sustainable programs that empower and change lives.

Zara Aina confronts the lack of educational opportunities facing children in Madagascar by using theatrical storytelling and performance to enable at-risk children to recognize their potential. We engage at-risk Malagasy children ages 10-14 years old who are prone to panhandling and homelessness. We create programming that addresses critical needs in the community such as communication and social skills.

At the heart of Zara Aina is the transformative power of storytelling. We believe that access to theater activities creates confident learners who are better prepared to participate actively in their education, community and social lives. From thinking creatively to collaborating with others, theatrical storytelling and performance can profoundly motivate children to achieve more and invest in their sense of possibility.

I have been a volunteer staff member since Zara Aina’s inception in 2012. I have helped work to create our programming, lead fundraising drives, apply for 501C3 tax exemption, as well as traveling to Madagascar as a volunteer teacher for the 2014 Trip.

Why Theater and Storytelling?

Through theater Zara Aina helps students of diverse backgrounds and abilities to channel their energies into inspiring artistic endeavors that foster creative expression, discipline, collaboration, self-awareness and personal transformation.

Students engaged in theater making learn to value their societies literary, oral and cultural traditions. They learn to express themselves and develop empathy for their own and other’s situations. Teaching and learning in theater develops confident learners who are better prepared to participate actively in their education.

Zara Aina’s pedagogy states that the children first and foremost are the storytellers; the children choose their own story, while the American facilitators use their theatrical training to help bring the theatrical embellishment to the story.

Through theatrical story telling and performance students will be able to: Think Creatively, Work with Others, Implement New Ideas, Reason Effectively, Communicate Clearly, Use Systems Thinking.

2014 Trip

Each year Zara Aina holds a month long program that works with at-risk Malagasy children ages 10-14 years old in performance skills, creating original dramatic work, theater vocabulary and literacy with the use of Malagasy folktales and theater. The goal is to use theatrical storytelling and performance as an opportunity for these children to develop self-worth, discipline, teamwork, and invest in one’s value of imagination.

In 2014 I traveled with Zara Aina to Antananarivo, Madagscar to volunteer as a teacher for Zara Aina. The US volunteers worked non-stop with 30 bright little stars to create a performance celebrating the 30th anniversary of UNICEF in Madagascar. The kids CRUSHED IT and toured the show all over the capital. Everyone who saw them to recognized the transformational power of storytelling and performance.

As a teacher in Zara Aina my primary goals were to address each child’s emotional, educational and developmental needs; create a sale and stable environment to explore ideas, improvise and develop self-worth; and to encourage taking full responsibility of one’s creative process.

The month long program culminated in a tour to several impoverished communities around Madagascar, where Zara Aina’s American volunteers have a chance to expand their outreach, conduct workshops, distribute school supplies, and perform their material. This year Zara Aina returned to conduct workshops in Andavabatu and Ranamofana, while also partnering with the Vohary Project in Morandava.. Each community has their own distinct personality.

Nearly with all the kids I worked with advances in discipline, attention span, and self-confidence were apparent. Some kids, once soft-spoken or shy, became increasingly louder and more articulate both in using their voices and bodies on stage; they began to take risks, present their own ideas about how their show should look, feel, and sound, and challenged themselves to create more expressively. They became accustomed to structured rehearsals and warm-ups.

At the end of the program one of the boys, Andreas, expressed to us that in the beginning he felt small and as if he had nothing to offer, but as a result of the time spent working with the company he felt “big” and recognized the gifts that he possesses. Moreover, there was an eagerness to share those gifts with others.

The most valuable thing I took away from my time in Madagascar was that these children, some of whom didn’t even have a place to call home, learned to value themselves as unique gifts and assets to each other and to their communities.

Andavabatu

Andavabatu is a farming community on the center Plateau of Madagascar. There is no running water or electricity. The school has one teacher for roughly 200 kids, aged 4-12. There is no arts education. The progress I saw during our week with the community during our workshop was amazing. While some children on the first day were scared to participate in activites, by the last day they were jumping into the work and fully expressing themselves. Before leaving, Zara Aina helped to build 200 new desks for the school, along with providing the materials to fix leaks in the school house roof.

Ranamofana

Ranamofana is in the heart of the rainforest and home to some of the worlds rarest wildlife. Zara Aina partnered with, center Valbio to hold a storytelling workshop with the local school. The work in this workshop was centered on developing a story with a beginning, middle, and end. The kids were also led in excercizes to help create character. The workshop culminated in a play the kids created about the rainforest holding a party for one lucky lemur, Nasty.

Morandava

In Morandava, a beautiful beach community, Zara Aina partnered with the Vohary Project. The Vohary Project is a local dance and storytelling club that focuses on creatively engaging young woman who would otherwise be prone to prostitution. Zara Aina’s work with the Vohary Project sparked a full-on traveling band of stories, songs, and cultural exchange.
